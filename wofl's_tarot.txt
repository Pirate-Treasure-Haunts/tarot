B) Expanded Aspects of the Psyche (Drawing from Modern Philosophy/Psychology)
Beyond the traditional archetypes, here are expanded psychological aspects, influenced by Jung, Nietzsche, Freud, Lacan, Foucault, and Sartre, that could be represented in a more expansive Tarot:

The Shadow (Jung): The unconscious aspects of the self that the conscious ego does not identify with. It's the "dark side" – repressed desires, weaknesses, instincts, and even underdeveloped positive qualities.
Tarot manifestation: Could be a card representing hidden fears, unacknowledged potential, or the necessity of confronting one's own darkness for integration.

The Persona (Jung): The mask we wear, the social role we present to the world. It's how we want to be perceived, often shielding our true selves.
Tarot manifestation: A card exploring authenticity vs. performativity, social conformity, or the pressures of public image.

The Will to Power (Nietzsche): Not necessarily aggressive power, but the fundamental drive to overcome, grow, affirm life, and actualize one's potential. It's a creative, driving force.
Tarot manifestation: A card about ambition, overcoming limitations, self-mastery, asserting one's values, or the inherent drive towards growth.

The Repressed Unconscious (Freud): Thoughts, memories, and desires that are kept out of conscious 
awareness due to their traumatic or unacceptable nature, yet continue to influence behavior. Tarot manifestation: A card symbolizing buried memories, unresolved traumas, unconscious motivations, or the emergence of suppressed desires.

The Gaze (Lacan): The omnipresent, structuring force of how we are seen and judged by others (and by society's symbolic order), influencing our sense of self and subjectivity.
Tarot manifestation: A card exploring external validation, social anxiety, self-consciousness, the impact of societal expectations, or the feeling of being observed/judged.

The Symbolic Order (Lacan): The realm of language, law, social norms, and cultural structures that define our reality and shape our identity. It's where meaning is constructed.
Tarot manifestation: A card representing societal rules, the structures of power, the influence of language, or the struggle to define oneself within established frameworks.

Discipline/Power-Knowledge (Foucault): The subtle and pervasive ways power operates through institutions, language, and knowledge systems, shaping bodies, minds, and behaviors, often without overt coercion.
Tarot manifestation: A card exploring systems of control, institutional norms, self-regulation, the impact of historical narratives, or the subtle pressures to conform.

Authenticity/Bad Faith (Sartre): Authenticity is living in accordance with one's freedom and responsibility, confronting anxiety. Bad Faith is denying one's freedom, pretending to be a predetermined "thing," avoiding responsibility.
Tarot manifestation: A card representing existential choice, embracing radical freedom, confronting anxiety, or the act of self-deception and evasion of responsibility.

The Absurd (Camus/Existentialism): The fundamental disharmony between humanity's search for meaning and the universe's lack of inherent meaning.
Tarot manifestation: A card dealing with finding meaning in a meaningless world, embracing futility, rebellion against meaninglessness, or the courage to create one's own values.

The Other (Lévinas/Existentialism): The encounter with another person in their radical difference and singularity, which can challenge and expand one's own subjective world.
Tarot manifestation: A card about encountering difference, empathy, ethical responsibility to the other, or the profound impact of genuine human connection.

Absolutely, fren! This is where the true magic of Tarot design comes alive – matching profound psychological concepts with evocative, subtly unsettling, and deeply symbolic imagery that taps into that "Ninth Gate" vibe. We want images that feel ancient, slightly arcane, and hint at hidden truths.

Here are 10 medieval traditional archetypes, or concepts that could be rendered in a traditional, slightly sinister/left-of-field style, to represent your expanded aspects of the psyche:

Expanded Archetypes for a Deeper Tarot
The Shadow (Jung):

Archetype: The Masked Penitent / The Cloaked Confessor
Imagery: A figure in a heavy, featureless cloak, hunched or kneeling, their face obscured by a dark, simple mask or deeply cast shadows. Perhaps they hold a single, extinguished lamp or a broken mirror fragment that reflects nothing. The background could be a desolate, shadowed corner of a forgotten church or a lightless cave entrance.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The anonymity and self-imposed darkness speak to the hidden, unacknowledged parts of self. The "penitent" aspect hints at suppressed guilt or aspects demanding recognition, while the "confessor" suggests the internal dialogue with what is buried. It's the part of you that observes from the shadows.
The Persona (Jung):

Archetype: The Jester's Visage / The Guild Master's Emblem
Imagery: A finely carved, ornate wooden mask, perhaps on a stand or held aloft by unseen hands, positioned before a simple, unassuming face or a blank space. The mask is intricately detailed, perhaps depicting a specific social role (a merchant, a knight, a scholar) but with subtly unsettling, unblinking eyes. Behind it, the reality is plain, unadorned.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The mask itself is not sinister, but the implication that it replaces or hides a true self is. The subtle "unblinking eyes" on the mask can feel like a constant performance, hinting at the effort and potential cost of maintaining the Persona. The Guild Master's emblem suggests the rigid identities society imposes.
The Will to Power (Nietzsche):

Archetype: The Iron Forger / The Ascent of the Serpent
Imagery: A powerful, determined figure (not overtly regal, but focused) actively shaping raw, glowing metal on an anvil, sparks flying. Their face is grimly determined, almost primal. Alternatively, a serpent coiled tightly around a vertical staff or gnarled tree, slowly, relentlessly, pushing upwards, its scales glinting with an internal light.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The raw, unyielding nature of the iron and the primal, relentless drive of the serpent suggest a force that can be creative or destructive, beautiful or terrifying in its singular pursuit of overcoming. It's the unflinching drive beneath polite society.
The Repressed Unconscious (Freud):

Archetype: The Sunken Crypt / The Whispering Well
Imagery: A dark, overgrown crypt door, half-submerged in murky, still water, with barely visible, ancient runes or scratch marks on its surface. Or, a deep, dark well with a single, faint, shimmering light at the very bottom, and spectral forms or barely audible voices seeming to emanate from its depths.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The "sunken" or "deep" aspect implies something buried and forgotten, yet still exerting a chilling influence. The murkiness and whispers evoke the fragmented, often disturbing nature of repressed memories and desires bubbling up. It's the past that refuses to stay buried.
The Gaze (Lacan):

Archetype: The Unblinking Eye / The Stained Glass Witness
Imagery: A single, large, disembodied eye, perhaps floating in a void or positioned high in an oppressive architectural space, its pupil fixed on the viewer. It's not a malevolent eye, but one of cold, objective observation, perhaps with slight distortions in its reflection. Or, a section of a stained-glass window depicting a crowd of obscured, faceless figures, all subtly turned towards and observing the space outside the frame.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The disembodied, unblinking nature of the eye is inherently unnerving, representing the pervasive, non-personal nature of societal judgment. The stained glass implies a divine or institutional judgment, the sense of being constantly under scrutiny from an anonymous collective.
The Symbolic Order (Lacan):

Archetype: The Script of the Scroll / The Grand Compiler's Tome
Imagery: An ancient, unfurled parchment scroll stretching endlessly into the distance, covered in tiny, meticulously uniform script that seems to define everything within the frame, but the script itself is unreadable or alien. Or, a vast, heavy, chained tome, pages filled with diagrams, laws, and classifications that seem to order the entire world, yet its authority feels absolute and unyielding.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The "unreadable" or "alien" script implies a system of meaning that is powerful and all-encompassing, yet fundamentally detached from individual experience. The chained tome speaks to the rigidity and inescapable nature of established laws and norms, subtly suggesting their power to bind.
Discipline/Power-Knowledge (Foucault):

Archetype: The Clockwork Overseer / The Panopticon Guard
Imagery: An intricate, self-operating clockwork mechanism, perhaps with fine, sharp gears and levers, subtly controlling the movements of small, humanoid automatons or even the growth of plants in a controlled environment. No human hand is visible, only the precise, relentless machinery. Alternatively, a lone, central tower in a round prison, its single, unseen guard implicitly observing all cells, creating self-discipline.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The cold, mechanical precision suggests power that is impersonal and pervasive, operating through systems rather than overt force. The lack of a visible controller makes it more unsettling, highlighting the internalized nature of modern discipline. The panopticon speaks to the power of unseen observation.
Authenticity/Bad Faith (Sartre):

Archetype: The Puppet's Strings / The Actor's Mirror
Imagery: A figure, perhaps a nobleman or merchant, seated, but from behind, thin, almost invisible strings are subtly attached to their limbs, leading upwards out of the frame. The figure is outwardly composed, but a slight tension in the strings or a subtle tremor reveals the hidden manipulation. Or, an actor gazing into a mirror, but the reflection shows not their true face, but a succession of masks or roles.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The "puppet" imagery is inherently unsettling, hinting at a lack of genuine agency or self-deception. The mirror reflecting only masks speaks to the evasion of true self, the constant performance of "being" something you are not, which is a form of self-betrayal.
The Absurd (Camus/Existentialism):

Archetype: The Sisyphus Stone / The Broken Compass
Imagery: A solitary, determined figure pushing a massive, perfectly spherical stone up an endless, barren incline under a vast, empty sky. The figure's face is stoic, neither joyful nor despairing. Or, a traditional, finely crafted compass lying on a desolate, featureless plain, its needle spinning wildly and uselessly, pointing nowhere.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The endless, seemingly pointless labor of Sisyphus is the epitome of the absurd, yet the figure's determined stance hints at the dignity of conscious rebellion. The broken compass, rather than merely being useless, actively mocks the search for direction, suggesting a fundamental lack of inherent purpose in the landscape it surveys.
The Other (Lévinas/Existentialism):

Archetype: The Unveiled Visage / The Threshold Glimmer
Imagery: Two figures, subtly different in dress or posture, facing each other across a stark, empty space. One figure (the "Other") has their face partially illuminated, not by direct light, but by an internal luminescence that makes them distinctly unique, almost unsettling in their singular presence, while the other figure (the "self") is slightly shadowed, observing. Or, a narrow threshold or doorway leading into an abyss of darkness, but from within the darkness, a single, distinct human face is barely visible, compelling in its mysterious singularity.
"Sinister/Left-of-Field" Feel: The "unveiled visage" isn't threatening, but its radical otherness can be disorienting or challenging to the ego. The internal luminescence suggests a self-contained reality that cannot be fully assimilated or understood, making the encounter both profound and slightly intimidating. The "threshold glimmer" makes the "Other" appear from a place beyond our grasp, emphasizing their uncontainable uniqueness.
These archetypes aim to maintain the traditional Tarot's symbolic richness while injecting that "Ninth Gate" sense of ancient, subtly unnerving wisdom that speaks to deeper, less comfortable truths about the human condition.
